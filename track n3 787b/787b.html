|-Mazda 787B

 The Mazda 787B is one of the most iconic and unique race cars in the history of motorsport, and it holds a special place in the hearts of car enthusiasts and racing fans worldwide. This sports prototype is best known for being the first and only rotary-powered car to win the prestigious 24 Hours of Le Mans, a feat it achieved in 1991. The 787B's victory remains a defining moment in racing history and cemented Mazda's legacy as a manufacturer willing to take bold engineering risks to achieve success.

|-History and Development

The Mazda 787B was developed by Mazda to compete in the Group C racing category during the late 1980s and early 1990s. Group C was a top-tier racing series that featured highly advanced prototype cars, and it was renowned for pushing the limits of engineering, speed, and endurance. Mazda's involvement in Group C racing began in the 1980s, with the company gradually developing a reputation for its innovative use of the rotary engine — a unique and unconventional engine design that had both pros and cons when compared to traditional piston engines.

Mazda had already made waves in motorsport with the 787 (a predecessor to the 787B) and the earlier 757 and 767 models, but the 787B represented a significant leap forward. The car was designed to be a high-performance machine capable of competing with the likes of Porsche, Mercedes-Benz, and Jaguar, all of whom were dominant forces in Group C racing at the time.

To ensure the 787B’s competitiveness, Mazda partnered with Mazda’s motorsport division, Mazda Motorsport (Mazda Speed), and assembled a team of top engineers, mechanics, and drivers to develop the car. The result was a lightweight, highly aerodynamic race car that could handle the demanding 24-hour endurance race at Le Mans.

Engine and Performance

The Mazda 787B is perhaps most famous for its rotary engine, specifically the 4-rotor Wankel rotary engine that powered the car to victory at the 1991 24 Hours of Le Mans. This engine, known as the R26B, was a major departure from the typical piston engines used by most of Mazda's competitors.

The R26B engine featured four rotors, which were a configuration rarely used in motorsport at the time. It produced an impressive 700 horsepower at 9,000 rpm, and despite being smaller and lighter than a typical piston engine of similar output, the rotary engine was capable of delivering exceptional power and high-revving performance. The engine’s relatively simple design and compact size also made it lighter and more efficient than many traditional engines, which was crucial for a race car that had to endure the grueling 24-hour race.

The 4-rotor Wankel engine was paired with a 5-speed manual transmission, and the car's power was sent to the rear wheels. The rotary engine’s ability to rev quickly and maintain consistent power throughout the race was a key factor in the 787B's success at Le Mans. One of the notable features of the rotary engine was its smoothness and distinctive sound — a high-pitched whine that was unique to Mazda's rotary-powered cars.

The 787B’s lightweight construction, combined with the high power-to-weight ratio of the engine, allowed the car to achieve blistering acceleration and impressive top speeds. It was capable of reaching speeds over 200 mph (320 km/h) on the straights of Le Mans, and its endurance over the 24-hour race was unparalleled, allowing it to maintain performance even during the toughest conditions.
